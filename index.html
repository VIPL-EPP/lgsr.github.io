Abstract

World models have emerged as powerful technology for facilitating policy learning in robotics by providing predictive representations of environmental dynamics. A crucial component of such models is the internal state representation, which serves as a bridge between observation, decision-making, and future state prediction. However, in multi-task settings, the presence of task-irrelevant information within the representation, especially under limited information capacity, can significantly impair the effectiveness of policy learning.

To address this challenge, we propose Language-Guided State Representation (LGSR), a novel method that leverages language-based task descriptions as priors to guide the encoding of state representations within the world model. By incorporating language instructions, LGSR promotes the extraction of task-relevant features while suppressing irrelevant information, thereby enhancing the alignment between representation learning and task objectives.

We evaluate LGSR on the CALVIN benchmark, a suite of language-conditioned robotic manipulation tasks. Experimental results demonstrate that incorporating language priors into the state representation enhances the extraction of task-relevant information, thereby significantly improving the efficiency and success rate of downstream policy learning.

Method

Let the task decide what the state should remember.

LGSR injects language at the encoder stage, compressing visual observations into a task-conditioned state before the world model predicts the future and the policy predicts actions.

Representation Analysis

What survives a one-token language-guided bottleneck? An inverse decoder lifts the frozen one-token LGSR back into MAE feature space before the same frozen MAE decoder reconstructs the scene.

Fine appearance details are discarded, but the language-specified block—or the drawer itself—remains. This directly supports the claim that MPE spends limited state capacity on task-relevant scene content.

Architecture

Transformer Inputs and Prediction Slots

At timestep i, the transformer receives a context block Aᵢ = [language, proprioception, visual state] followed by prediction slots Bᵢ = [mₒ, mₐ]. Ground-truth future observations and actions are decoding targets, never input tokens. World-model and policy features are produced by one shared forward pass.

  2. Prediction slots are sinks. Context and future slots cannot read prediction tokens, preventing target leakage.

Inference: use the same mask tokens, decode the latest action chunk, execute its first action, advance history by one observation, and repeat. The future decoder is an auxiliary training signal; control is produced from the action feature.

Instruction Annotation Protocol

This protocol supplies the Goal, Constraint, Action, Spatial, Logic, and Other labels used by the instruction-component masking ablation shown in Results. Each deduplicated CALVIN instruction is labeled automatically, loaded into a span-colored viewer, and checked or corrected by a human annotator. During the ablation, only the corresponding spans in the LGSR encoder’s instruction are masked; the policy always receives the full instruction, isolating representation-level effects.

Read the full annotation prompt 
[SYSTEM]
You are an expert annotator for robot-instruction semantics.
You must label tokens/spans into exactly five classes:
- G (Goal): target objects or entities (the noun head of the target NP).
- C (Constraint): attributes, colors, quantities, determiners, ordinal words that restrict G or A.
- A (Action): executable actions (verbs and their particles), e.g., "pick up", "take off".
- S (Spatial): spatial/positional/relational expressions (prepositions, directional adverbs, PP chunks).
- L (Logic): sequencing/logic connectors ("and", "then", "if", "before", "after", ...).
Tokens that fall into none of the five classes are tagged O.

Rules:
1) Prefer span-level labeling with contiguous spans. Multiword expressions like "pick up", "in front of" must be a single span.
2) Within a noun phrase, the head noun is G; its modifiers (color/quantity/ordinal) are C.
3) Spatial phrases that modify G or motion are S (e.g., "in the drawer", "towards the door").
4) Logic words are L and never overlap with A/G/C/S.
5) If a pronoun clearly refers to the current G (e.g. "pick it up"), label the pronoun "it" as G.
6) Output both SPANS and BIO:
   - SPANS: a list of {start, end, label, text} where start/end are token indices (end exclusive).
   - TOKENS: the tokenized sequence.
   - BIO: BIO tags aligned with TOKENS (B-*, I-*, or O); BIO must reflect the same spans.

Tokenization: split by whitespace and punctuation, but keep underscores (e.g., "pick_up"). Do not merge spans across non-adjacent tokens.
Return JSON with keys "tokens", "spans", "bio". No commentary.

What transfers—and what saturates?

Encoder-stage language fusion transfers: scratch LGSR reaches 82.3%, 3.6 points above scratch Seer. The proxy loss does not improve LIBERO-Long (82.0% versus 82.3%) because the representation probe is already saturated at 100%. On CALVIN, where scenes are shared across tasks, the same auxiliary adds 0.18 Avg. Len. This is why its contribution is described as benchmark-conditional.

Attention Visualization Experiments

Feature-basis probe

A PCA probe first compares the spatial structure of the candidate feature bases. MAE’s pixel-reconstruction features are distributed across the image, while DINOv2 separates objects and coherent scene regions more clearly.

Why DINOv2 for visualization?

Because its feature regions are spatially coherent, DINOv2 provides a more legible basis for localizing the response of the learned representation. This choice is limited to the visualization probe; LGSR training and quantitative evaluation continue to use the MAE encoder.

DINOv2 attention visualization

The overlays compare the same DINOv2 probe across the raw scene, LGSR without language guidance, and full LGSR, revealing where task conditioning changes the probe response.

Scope and Limitations

Successful demonstrations

The reward head is trained only on successful expert clips. Failure rollouts are out of distribution, so the auxiliary is not a calibrated reward for arbitrary policy states.

Alignment requirement

Time-to-go needs a language-aligned clip endpoint. Open-ended or unsegmented tasks would require task-specific milestones such as contact, half-open state, or release.

Benchmark dependence

The proxy objective helps on CALVIN but not on structurally saturated LIBERO-Long. Language fusion is the transferable contribution; the auxiliary benefit depends on task and scene structure.
